# Homepage Strategy

> Source: live audit of https://www.jeremylaycock.com.au/ (homepage, About page, AI page, and the PageUp case study as a representative sample) plus researched industry best practice for senior UX/Product Design portfolios. See `docs/current-focus.md` for the active phase this supports and `docs/project-vision.md` for the goals this is measured against.

## Current Homepage Analysis

**Structure (top to bottom):** nav header → hero → flat grid of 11 projects → footer.

**Hero copy (verbatim):** *"UX/UI product designer"* — *"I am passionate about improving digital products so people can interact with them naturally and intuitively. I also strive to create meaningful and lasting connections through UI and visual design."*

**Navigation:** UX/UI design, Brand identity design, About me, AI, Contact.

**Projects (11, presented with equal visual weight, no curation):** Open Universities Australia (NDA — case study inaccessible), Restore for Retail, ACEM CPD Portal, Goodwork, Restore Photo Markup Tool, VicSuper, VeroGuard/W3D, PageUp, ANZ Assured, MYER, Beeline.

**Case study depth (PageUp, sampled as representative):** clear chronological process narrative (problem → MVP flows → design exploration → visual language → developer handoff → launch), ~70% imagery / 30% text. Problem framing is concrete: *"Employees found logging into a system to consume formal company driven content archaic, impersonal and not relevant."* Outcome statement is qualitative only: *"the business was wrapped with how it turned out."* No research methodology, no testing evidence, no metrics.

**CTAs / footer:** "Say hi over email," CV download, LinkedIn.

**Differentiator already present:** a dedicated "AI in Design" page — thought-leadership content (references Jared Spool, the IxDF "AI for Designers" course, and hands-on use of Claude Code), positioned as a philosophy piece rather than a project. Currently buried as a fifth-level nav item.

**About page:** a genuinely distinctive personal narrative — creative roots in murals and skateboard art, through Graphic Communication study, into UX — not currently surfaced anywhere on the homepage.

## Key Findings & Opportunities

**Strengths**
- Deep, varied body of work (11 projects across fintech, health, retail, HR, and cybersecurity) — real evidence of the 10+ years' seniority claimed in the vision.
- A genuine, hard-to-copy differentiator already exists (the AI-in-Design POV and personal creative-origin story) — it's just not being used on the homepage.
- Clean, minimal visual execution with no clutter.

**Weaknesses**
- The hero is generic and interchangeable with almost any mid-level UX designer's — it leads with a job title and a mission-statement sentence, not a point of view, an outcome, or anything that signals Lead/Senior-level strategic thinking.
- Case studies are process-heavy and outcome-light. This is a direct contradiction of the vision's own success criterion — *"present case studies as compelling stories with measurable outcomes"* — the sampled case study has none.
- No results-first framing anywhere: outcomes (where they exist at all) are buried at the end of a long process narrative, the opposite of how time-constrained recruiters actually scan.
- Zero social proof — no testimonials, no client logos, nothing that shortcuts trust for a hiring manager skimming quickly.
- Flat, uncurated grid of 11 equally-weighted projects. For a Lead Product Designer pitch, presenting everything with equal weight reads as a portfolio archive, not a curated argument for seniority.
- The top-listed project (Open Universities Australia) is NDA-blocked with no case study — a dead end as the very first thing a visitor might click.

**Missed opportunities**
- Nothing on the homepage speaks to leadership, mentoring, cross-functional influence, or business strategy — despite the vision explicitly wanting to demonstrate "product strategy" and "systems thinking" to design leaders and product leaders.
- The AI-in-Design differentiator and the personal creative-origin story are both strong, distinctive assets that are currently invisible until several clicks deep.

## Competitive Comparison

Researched best practice for senior/product design portfolios (Case Study Club, Sitebuilder Report, IxDF, and related UX-writing sources — see Sources below) converges on a few consistent points relevant here:

- Recruiters and hiring managers should be able to understand a designer's strengths **within minutes** — favoring scannable structure, short sections, and strong headings over dense process documentation.
- The strongest case studies **lead with outcomes/results before the full narrative**, because reviewers are time-constrained and results earn the attention needed to read further.
- Senior-level portfolios differentiate themselves by communicating **strategic thinking, real-world constraints, and cross-team impact** — not just polished screens.
- Curated project selection (a handful of flagship pieces) reads as more senior than an exhaustive archive.

Against this, the current homepage under-indexes on outcome framing, curation, and strategic signaling, while over-indexing on volume of process artifacts relative to narrative payoff — the opposite of what the research indicates works for the exact audience (recruiters, hiring managers, design/product leaders) named in the vision.

## Three Strategic Concepts

### Concept 1 — Outcomes-First Strategist

Replace the generic hero with a positioning statement built around impact, and cut the project grid from 11 to 4–6 flagship case studies, each teased with a one-line **outcome**, not just a title. Case studies themselves get restructured to lead with results, per the results-first pattern above.

*Rationale:* Most directly fixes the weaknesses found — generic hero, no outcomes, no curation — and maps precisely onto the vision's own success criteria ("value within the first minute," "measurable outcomes").

### Concept 2 — Narrative & Craft

Lead the homepage with the personal creative-origin story (murals, skateboard art, Graphic Communication) as the brand hook, paired with strong visual craft. Case studies are restructured as story arcs (challenge → journey → resolution) in a confident, human voice matching the vision's "modern, confident, understated, premium" tone.

*Rationale:* Turns a genuinely distinctive, hard-to-copy asset (the personal story) into differentiation from every other "passionate UX/UI designer" homepage. Risk: a story-led hero may under-communicate seniority and impact to a recruiter scanning in under a minute.

### Concept 3 — Systems & Leadership

Restructure the homepage around capability themes (e.g. "Complex systems design," "Design leadership," "Design at scale in regulated industries") rather than a flat chronological project grid, and elevate the AI-in-Design POV from a buried nav item to a homepage-level proof point of forward-thinking leadership.

*Rationale:* Speaks most directly to the "design leaders / product leaders" audience segment in the vision and makes full use of the AI differentiator. Risk: capability-based framing is less immediately scannable for recruiters less fluent in UX taxonomy than for design-leader peers.

## Recommended Direction

**Concept 1 (Outcomes-First Strategist), as the primary structure** — it most directly resolves the specific weaknesses the audit surfaced and is the best fit for the vision's stated success criteria and primary audience (recruiters and hiring managers first, who are the most time-constrained readers).

Fold in supporting elements from the other two rather than treating them as competing directions:
- From **Concept 2**: use the personal creative-origin story as a short, confident hook inside the hero subhead or immediately below it — texture and memorability without letting narrative override the outcomes-first structure.
- From **Concept 3**: surface the AI-in-Design POV and leadership/systems-thinking signals as supporting proof points below the flagship projects, rather than restructuring the entire homepage around capability taxonomy (which risks legibility for the recruiter audience).

This gives one coherent homepage — outcomes and curation doing the primary persuasion work, personal story and leadership signals doing the differentiation work underneath it.
